Predicting Life Expectancy Based on Lifestyle

Developed by Nikita Prerna Toppo(BE/10322/17) Asha Tirkey(BE/10578/16) and Kavita Tigga(BE/10489/17) under the guidance of Dr. Sabina Priyadarshini.

OBJECTIVE

To predict life expectancy based on lifestyle and ways to increase life expectancy.

WHY CALCULATE ONE'S LIFE EXPECTANCY?

Some people find the concept of life expectancy calculator to be offensive. However, there are many reasons other than morbid curiosity for wanting to determine approximately how long one has to live.

For example, if an individual is terminally ill or has a progressive condition such as Alzheimer’s and they require long term care, their family will want them to live out their days in an environment where they are most comfortable. Knowing approximately how long care will be required will help the family make the decision whether they can afford to pay for Alzheimer’s care or whether they’ll need to qualify for Medicaid. One decision might be made with a life expectancy of 6 months and an entirely different decision with a life expectancy of several years. This is especially relevant when it comes to paying for long term care using financial products such as reverse mortgages that have high upfront costs.

ABOUT THIS TOOL

The Life Expectancy Tool is based on a statistical analysis project led by Dr. Sabina Priyadarshini at Birla Institute of Technology , Mesra , Ranchi. The Tool is based on self collected dataset of approximately 115 dead people in India ; mostly in the state of Jharkhand. The data was received in response to build a curated dataset for the project in hand. The data is anonymized for research purposes. Survey participants responded on behalf of their dead family members which included questions like medical history ,diet , health,lifestyle , etc. The date of their death, was also recorded.

Almost 500 people were sent surveys. The cohort consisted of 300 respondents. After cleaning the data (by removing respondents with extreme values of height, weight, caloric consumption, alcohol consumption,etc.), there were 200 survey responses left. Respondents whose dates of death were the same day as their reported survey responses were then removed.Further we removed the data of respondents who did not report currently having any chronic disease at the time of the survey, which left us with 113 total respondents. Of these, 67.3% were men and 32.7% were women.
